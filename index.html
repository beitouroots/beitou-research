🚨 Geopolitics

US Strikes Iran Again, Naval Blockade Back On, Oil Holding Near a One-Month High

  • The tea: US forces hit Iranian missile, drone and coastal-defense sites again Wednesday, and US Central Command reinstated the naval blockade of Iranian ports near the Strait of Hormuz the day before. 💥
  • The tape: crude barely budged on the fresh strikes because the escalation was already priced in. WTI closed Wednesday at $79.60 and Brent at $84.95, both near a one-month high and roughly 19% above where Brent traded before the US-Israel war on Iran began in late February. 🛢️
  • The backdrop: oil had briefly slipped back toward pre-war levels last month on hopes for a peace deal, but this week's strikes and the blockade have reversed a chunk of that decline. 📉
  • Why it matters: a grinding, not spiking, oil shock is the trickiest kind for the Fed. It's not dramatic enough to force a policy response, but it's sticky enough to keep energy costs elevated right as officials debate cutting rates. ⚖️
Sources Al Jazeera CNBC Bloomberg
02
🦅 The Fed

Rate Cut Odds Are Shifting to September, Not July, After Cooler CPI

  • The tea: markets are pricing roughly an 84.5% probability the Fed holds rates steady at its July 28-29 meeting, with the funds rate staying at 3.50%-3.75%, the same pause it's held since December. 🎙️
  • The setup: that hold call leans on last week's cooler prints, June CPI fell 0.4% month over month, bringing annual inflation down to 3.5%, while core CPI held flat at 2.6%. 🌶️
  • The catch: the June FOMC's own updated projections showed limited appetite for cuts this year, with the median year-end 2026 rate estimate ticking up to 3.8%, so traders are now watching the July CPI print and incoming data for any signal that September reopens the door. 📋
  • Why it matters: a Fed that's on hold into September means today's rally or selloff is being driven by earnings and oil, not by fresh rate-cut hopes. ⚖️
Sources Kiplinger CME FedWatch Data via BLS

🤖 Tech

TSMC Posts a Record Quarter, Raises Capex, and the Stock Falls Anyway

  • The tea: Taiwan Semiconductor (TSM) reported record second-quarter revenue and lifted its 2026 capex outlook to the high end of its $52 billion to $56 billion range to expand advanced-node capacity for AI chips. 🔬
  • The twist: instead of celebrating, the stock fell about 1.55% in premarket trading after the company also warned of higher chip prices ahead, a signal that even AI's biggest winner is passing rising costs down the line. 📉
  • The ripple: it's the second straight day of semiconductor weakness, dragging Nasdaq-100 futures down about 0.7% this morning as traders weigh whether pricier chips squeeze the AI buildout's margins. 🧨
  • Why it matters: TSMC just told the market the AI capex supercycle is real and accelerating, but it's also getting more expensive to buy into, and that tension is exactly what's rattling chip stocks today. 🏗️

🧠 The Big Brain · the 6-12 month bet
🧠 Deep Thoughts

AI's Biggest Supplier Just Told the Market Its Own Boom Is Getting More Expensive

  • The big idea: TSMC didn't miss, it raised capex to the high end of its range on record demand, and the stock still fell, because it also warned prices are going up. That's a subtle but important shift: the AI buildout is no longer just a demand story, it's becoming a cost story. 🎢
  • The tension: every hyperscaler and chipmaker riding the AI capex wave now has to absorb pricier wafers and equipment at the exact moment the Fed is on hold and oil is adding its own cost pressure from the Iran conflict (Story 1). Margins, not demand, are becoming the thing to watch. 🛢️
  • The trade: over the next 6 to 12 months, watch whether AI infrastructure spenders can pass rising chip costs through to customers the way TSMC just passed them upstream, or whether that squeeze starts showing up in hyperscaler margins by year-end. 🔬
  • TL;DR: today's tape is chip weakness, a UnitedHealth beat, and a Fed on hold, but the trade that could matter most by year-end is whether the AI trade can survive getting more expensive to build. 🔒

🧠 The Big Brain · the bottom line
🧠 Deep Thoughts

The AI Trade Is Splitting Into Winners and Losers Inside Its Own Supply Chain

  • The takeaway: today was not a referendum on AI, it was a referendum on which parts of the AI stack win. Apple's China approval and Alibaba's Qwen deal show the consumer-facing platform layer of AI still has real room to run, while Micron's slide on Chinese memory competition shows the hardware supply chain is getting genuinely contested by overseas rivals, not just US peers. 🔬
  • The tension: cooling wholesale inflation gives the Fed room to stay patient, but that macro tailwind is now competing with company-specific and country-specific stories, China's regulatory posture toward Apple on one side, China's memory-chip capacity on the other, that have nothing to do with rate policy. ⚖️
  • The trade: watch whether Micron's slide is a one-day scare or the start of a real re-rating of US memory makers against Chinese competition. That debate matters more to the semiconductor trade over the next 6 to 12 months than this week's inflation print. 🔍
  • TL;DR: Big Tech and cooling inflation carried the indices to fresh highs today, but underneath the surface, AI's supply chain just showed investors it has real geographic risk, and SpaceX's slide below its IPO price is a reminder that hype alone does not hold up a stock price forever. 🔒
