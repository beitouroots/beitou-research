🏭 Big Tech

Microsoft Cut 4,800 Jobs and Blew Up Xbox As We Know It

  • The tea: Microsoft (MSFT) is eliminating about 4,800 roles, roughly 2.1% of its workforce, with about 1,600 of today's cuts hitting Xbox. Gaming reductions are set to reach about 3,200 this fiscal year, around 20% of Xbox staff. The stock traded down 1.7% early. 🎮
  • The overhaul: four game studios are being spun off or sold and a fifth is under review, with Compulsion Games and Double Fine going independent again. It is the deepest restructuring in Xbox history. 🧨
  • The why: the company keeps shoveling capital into the AI race, and Chief People Officer Amy Coleman says the eliminated roles are NOT being replaced by AI. The market read: cost discipline funding capex. 💸
  • Why it matters: Friday's jobs report showed hiring stalling at a +36,000 monthly average. Big Tech trimming headcount while spending billions on chips IS the 2026 labor market in one headline. ⚖️

🛢️ Energy

OPEC+ Turns the Taps Up for a Fifth Straight Month

  • The tea: OPEC+ agreed Sunday to raise August output targets by 188,000 barrels per day, the fifth straight monthly increase. Under the allocation, Saudi Arabia adds 62,000 bpd to reach 10.4 million and Russia adds 62,000 to reach 9.88 million. 🛢️
  • The tape: oil barely blinked. WTI slipped 0.52% to $68.33 and Brent 0.50% to $71.76 in early Monday trade, with crude already sitting at pre-war price levels after last week's slide. 📉
  • The backdrop: flows through the Strait of Hormuz keep reviving, and the group is betting Middle East stability holds under the uneasy US-Iran truce. More barrels into a healing market keeps the pressure pointed down. 🕊️
  • Why it matters: cheap crude is the Fed's best friend right now. Every OPEC+ hike is another brick in the "inflation risks have come down" wall Warsh described last week. 🦅

🔔 The Close

No Bell Today, So Here Is Where the Week Officially Ended

  • The holiday: July 4th lands on a Saturday this year, so US stock and bond markets were closed all day today and reopen Monday, July 6. No close, no movers, no drama. The tiles above are Thursday's official finals, and they were stronger than the mid-afternoon tape suggested. 🎆
  • The official scoreboard: a late-day surge carried the Dow up 594.83 points, or 1.14%, to a record close of 52,900.07, its fourth straight weekly gain and longest streak since October 2024. The S&P 500 finished flat at 7,483.24, the Nasdaq fell 0.8% to 25,832.67, and the Russell 2000 dropped 1.08% to 2,980.05, back under 3,000. 🟣
  • The driver: June payrolls of just 57,000 versus the 115,000 expected, with April and May revised down a combined 74,000. Unemployment dipped to 4.2%, but mostly because participation slid to 61.5%, the lowest since March 2021. Per CME FedWatch, September rate-hike odds dimmed to 55% from 64.1%, and the 10-year yield eased to 4.47%. 📊

🧠 The Big Brain · the bottom line
🧠 Deep Thoughts

The Barbecue Test: A Market Learning to Rally Without Its Chips

  • The takeaway: this week was the whole regime in miniature. Thursday gave you a record Dow with 70% of the S&P advancing right next to a 5.4% semi wipeout, and then Friday, while America grilled, Seoul snapped back 5.8%. That is volatility inside a rotation, not the start of a crash. 🟣
  • The signal: the market is separating businesses that own AI demand from businesses that merely sell capacity into it. Meta renting out spare compute put the overcapacity question on the table, and Friday's Kospi rip does not answer it, it just proves how fast money floods back at the first hint of a floor. 🔄
  • The macro: payrolls have stalled to a 36,000-a-month average over the past year, inflation is still 4.2%, and oil at four-month lows is doing the Fed's work for it. Per CME FedWatch, September hike odds slid to 55%. A Fed boxed in on both sides leaves breadth and positioning driving the tape. 📊
  • TL;DR: the 6-to-12-month bet stays with the broadening: small caps, rate-sensitives, and the other 490 stocks. The July tells: bank earnings mid-month, whether memory pricing survives the overcapacity scare, and whether the SK Hynix listing prices into strength or fear. 🔒
