01
📊 Jobs Aftermath

57,000 Jobs Later, the Hike Trade Is Bleeding Out

  • The number: June payrolls rose just 57,000, way under the roughly 110,000 economists expected. Unemployment ticked down to 4.2%, but the rearview stayed ugly: April and May were revised down by a combined 74,000 jobs, and the 12-month average is now a skinny +36,000. 😬
  • Under the hood: leisure and hospitality shed 61,000 jobs on weak summer hiring, health care added 22,000 (slower than its 38,000 monthly average), and wages rose 0.3% on the month, 3.5% on the year, to $37.64 an hour. 🔍
  • The Fed math: per CME FedWatch, odds of a September rate HIKE dimmed to 55% from 64.1% after the print. As 50 Park's Adam Sarhan put it, the report "just takes the pressure off the Fed to raise rates in the short term." 🎲
  • Why it matters: with markets dark today, this is the number the bond market chews on all weekend. Monday's open prices a softer Fed. 🎆
Sources BLS (primary) Reuters CNBC
02
🏆 The Close

Official Scoreboard: Record Dow, Dead-Flat S&P, and a Tape Split in Two

  • The close: the Dow jumped 594.83 points, +1.14%, to a record 52,900.07. The S&P 500 finished at 7,483.24, up 0.01 points, as flat as a close gets. The Nasdaq fell 0.80% to 25,832.67 and the Russell 2000 slipped 0.55% to 2,996.11. 🏆
  • The streak: that is a fourth straight weekly gain for the Dow, its longest run since October 2024. For the week: Dow +2%, S&P 500 +1.8%, Nasdaq +2.1%. Not bad for a week with a jobs shocker in it. 📈
  • The hero: Apple (AAPL) rose 4.8% and helped carry all three big indexes after Nikkei Asia reported plans for five new iPhone models. Breadth was healthy too: NYSE advancers beat decliners 1.42 to 1. 🍎
  • Why it matters: a record Dow on a soft-jobs day is the market voting for rotation over panic. Everything reopens Monday. 🇺🇸
Sources Reuters TheStreet A. Guillot

💾 Semis

The Memory Massacre, Day Two: Chips Fell 5.4% Into the Fireworks

  • The tea: the semiconductor index dropped 5.4% Thursday, its second straight sharp fall. SanDisk (SNDK) sank another 14.1%, Nvidia (NVDA) eased 1.4%, and even after two brutal days the group is still up about 78% in 2026. 💾
  • The why: no earnings miss here. Granite Wealth's Bruce Zaro called it profit-taking after this year's monster run, and Citi put out a warning on semiconductor and hyperscaler names the same day. When everyone has already won, someone starts cashing chips. 🎰
  • The collateral: Tesla (TSLA) dropped 7.5% even though Q2 deliveries came in above estimates, a textbook sell-the-news fade after a sharp run-up into the print. Vimeo owner Bending Spoons fell 11.3% a day after its 40% Nasdaq debut pop. 📉
  • Why it matters: this selloff is a valuation argument, not a demand collapse, and those arguments get settled by the next round of AI capex numbers, not by a long weekend. 🔌
Sources Reuters TheStreet A. Guillot

🛢️ Geopolitics

Crude Just Round-Tripped the Entire War

  • The tape: oil fell for a third straight session Thursday, with WTI at $67.59, down 1.44%, and Brent at $70.60, down 1.36%, in Thursday trade. Brent touched its lowest level since late February: the war premium is basically gone. 🛢️
  • The supply: flows through the Strait of Hormuz are back above 10 million barrels a day, with the UAE restoring exports above 3.9 million bpd and Saudi Arabia ramping shipments to Asia. Meanwhile US and Iranian officials kept talking via Qatari mediators, and Trump said "the denuclearization of Iran is moving along well." 🕊️
  • The Fed angle: Chair Kevin Warsh said Wednesday that "inflation risks have come down," pointing straight at falling energy prices. May CPI ran 4.2%, the hottest since 2023, and crude is unwinding it in real time. 🦅
  • Why it matters: energy is the fastest-moving input into headline inflation. If Hormuz stays open, oil keeps doing the Fed's job for it. ⚖️

🍸 How the day actually went
01
🔔 The Close

Soft Jobs, Record Dow, and a Tape Split Right Down the Middle

  • The headline: America added just 57,000 jobs in June, a big miss against the 115,000 expected, and May was revised down to 129,000. Unemployment dipped to 4.2%, but mostly because participation slid to 61.5%, the lowest since March 2021. Wages rose 3.5% over the year. Soft, not scary. 📊
  • The tape: the market split in two. The Dow rose 0.69% to 52,668.64, a fresh record close, the S&P 500 was basically flat at -0.08%, while the Nasdaq fell 1.15% to 25,739.80 and the Russell 2000 dropped 1.3%. Breadth was the tell: roughly 70% of S&P names closed green, led by health care, staples and utilities, while tech dragged, with the tech sector fund down 2.6%. 🟣
  • The read: soft payrolls took the 2026 rate-hike chatter down a notch, and the 10-year yield eased to 4.46%. BMO's rates desk called a July hike hard to envision after this print. Markets are closed tomorrow for Independence Day and reopen Monday. 🦅
Sources BLS CNBC TheStreet
02
🟢 Winners & 🔴 Losers

Tesla Delivers a Record and Gets Punished Anyway

  • 🔴 Tesla (TSLA): the day's strangest chart. Tesla reported record Q2 deliveries of 480,126 vehicles, up 25% from a year ago and way past the 406,000 Wall Street expected, and the stock still sank about 7%, its worst day in nearly a year. Shares had run up double digits into the print, and TSLA has now fallen on each of its last three delivery reports. Sell the news, buy the fireworks. 🎆
  • 🔴 The AI complex: tech was the worst sector of the day. Meta (META) gave back almost 4% of Wednesday's big cloud-announcement pop, and the memory names that got smoked Wednesday stayed under pressure as the chip slide extended into a second day. 📉
  • 🟢 The quiet stuff: defensives finally got their day. Health care, consumer staples and utilities led, roughly 354 of the S&P 500's names advanced, and that broad strength is what carried the Dow to a record while the loud tickers bled. 💪
Sources CNBC TheStreet Yahoo Finance

💥 The Standout

The Meta Shock Goes Global: Seoul Has Its Worst Day in Years

  • The move: Wednesday's chip rout jumped the Pacific overnight. Korea's Kospi plunged 7.89% in what local press dubbed the Meta Shock, with SK Hynix cratering 14.57% and Samsung Electronics sinking 9.06% as sell orders poured into anything memory. US semis stayed heavy for a second straight day. 💥
  • The why: Meta's plan to sell its excess AI compute as a cloud business was great news for Meta and terrible news for everyone selling it chips. If the biggest AI spenders have spare capacity to rent out, the market's next question is whether they overbought, and that question landed hardest on the memory makers whose stocks had gone parabolic. Citi put out a caution flag on semis and hyperscalers the same day. 🔀
  • The stakes: awkward timing alert: SK Hynix has been planning a $29 billion Nasdaq listing as soon as July 10, per CNBC. Whether that lands into a stabilized tape or a deepening overcapacity scare is now one of July's biggest storylines. 🎢

🧠 Deep Thoughts

The Great Re-Sort: Who Actually Owns the AI Trade

  • The takeaway: today was two markets in one. A record Dow with 70% of the S&P advancing on one screen, and a global AI-infrastructure unwind on the other, from Micron to Seoul. That is not a crash, that is a re-sort. 🟣
  • The signal: Meta renting out spare AI compute is the first hyperscaler effectively admitting it may hold more capacity than it needs. The market is now separating businesses that own AI demand from businesses that merely sell capacity into it, and the memory names that went parabolic are the first casualties of that question. 🔄
  • The macro: jobs growth has stalled to a 36,000-a-month average over the past year while oil sits at four-month lows doing the Fed's inflation-fighting for it. A Fed that can neither hike nor cut leaves breadth and positioning driving the tape, and right now the money is broadening out. 📊
